The World Inside Our Home


Born in the UK, made in the world


HUMANS, unlike spiders, could spin a complex web of trade routes across the world to share food. And they have done so for hundreds of years. The map to your left illustrates a small part of that web: trade flows from 180 countries and territories into the UK in 2017. These trade routes carried about 40.8 million tonnes of food and 40 million live animals worth £43.5 billion. This figure is equivalent to the combined six months’ worth of groceries of all British families.

Hover over the lines or circles on the map to see import details for each country.

Suppose we cook a dish called cawl, a Welsh word for soup or broth and Wales’s national dish. In north Wales, it is also called lobscows.

A bowl of this dish may be linked to an estimated 153 countries and territories, which supplied the UK with 2.5 million tonnes of food and 23 live animals in 2017. These imports were valued at £4.6 billion.

Zoe Jones and Margaret Davis, restaurant owners in the sleepy villages of Manorbier and Laugharne in Pembrokeshire, serve this traditional Welsh food. Although one’s recipe may differ from that of the other, both agree on one thing: cawl is cooked with Welsh lamb and extended by root vegetables. Leeks, potatoes, carrots, swedes, parsnips, cabbages, and onions find their way into cawl. Herbs like thyme may also be added. Cawl is traditionally served with slices of bread and slivers of cheese to be sprinkled over it.

Check which of the 153 countries are potential suppliers of these ingredients.

But Redvers du Wayne, a retired chef from Laugharne, and Tiffany Edwards and Sharon Harwood, locals of Tenby, tell me that Welsh lamb has become expensive. For good measure, Wayne adds, lamb from New Zealand is tender and delicious but costs less than Welsh lamb. That’s what his recipe of cawl has been made with for decades, he reveals. Some locals, like Edwards, prefer bacon or ham, while others opt for beef.

Choose a recipe for your cawl, and on a bowl, let’s trace the strands of food trade web connecting us to countries around the world, one ingredient at a time.

Historian Martin Wright from the Cardiff University says, “all nations developed their own range of food based on basically practical concerns.” Traditional food, like cawl, is “a direct result of what was available and easy to harvest.”

When I travelled to Wales’s scenic countryside, I saw hardly a mountain or lush green plain that’s undotted with sheep. The rural uplands, which form the greater part of Wales, can only support livestock farming, Wright confirms. Wales is the peninsula jutting out of western Britain. It is a mass of mountains that has influenced the country’s food. The absence of grain and a variety of vegetables explains its people’s dependence on meat and animal products.

The various ingredients of a cawl recipe indicate how changes in the global food system have ensured a wide selection of products, from New Zealand lamb to Danish bacon to Turkish cheeses, and seasonal food, such as apples and broccoli, to be available to us all year round.
